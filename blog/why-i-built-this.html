Why I Built This: Vibe Coding, AI Paranoia, and the Website That Started It All

A welcome letter, a confession about imposter syndrome, and a brief explanation of what vibe coding actually is — and why it sent me down a rabbit hole at midnight.

March 4, 2026

Dear reader,

Welcome to my new page.

If you're here, you probably know me — or you stumbled across this from LinkedIn, or from some corner of the internet where I've been quietly building things. Either way, I'm glad you're here. This is the first post on this site, and it's a bit of a confession.

Let me explain why this website exists at all.

The Wave I Almost Missed

Sometime in late 2025, it became very clear that the AI wave was not going to wait for anyone to get comfortable. Not for organisations. Not for white-collar workers. Not for strategy consultants turned managers. Not for me.

The conversation had shifted from "AI might change things eventually" to "AI is actively eliminating entire categories of knowledge work right now." The vibe coding discourse alone — the idea that you no longer need to know how to code to build software — was making rounds in every tech circle, startup community, and corporate hallway I was part of.

And I'll be honest: I felt like I was falling behind. Not catastrophically. But enough to notice.

I was reading about it. Nodding along in meetings. But I wasn't doing it. And there's a difference.

Then I got promoted.

New Role, New Imposter

In March 2026, I became Senior Manager working with Product Management and AI Business Transformation at Norlys. Which is a long title that essentially means: help drive AI initiatives into the business — for colleagues and for customers — while also running product management across our channels.

The imposter syndrome arrived before I did.

I came from strategy consulting. My mental model of "getting things done" was structured around PowerPoint decks, waterfall projects, PRINCE2 governance, and extremely carefully worded executive recommendations. Which is all fine and useful — right up until the moment someone asks you to lead a product organisation and run an AI transformation and you realise you've spent years advising on these things without ever actually doing them.

So I did what any self-aware person with imposter syndrome does: I went on a certification spree.

First stop: PSPO I — Professional Scrum Product Owner, certified by Scrum.org.

I used to think product owners were just project managers who had rebranded. I was wrong. I am now a product owner. Jokes on me.

Coming from old-school consulting — where waterfall was gospel and the Gantt chart was sacred — I was not expecting to find Scrum interesting. But I did. Genuinely. The logic of short feedback cycles, working software over documentation, and empowered teams who can say no to bad scope — it clicked in a way that years of PRINCE2 never quite did. The framework is simple enough to start with and deep enough to spend years improving at. I get it now.

The certification didn't cure the imposter syndrome. But it made me a better-prepared imposter. And then I discovered Claude Code.

What is Vibe Coding?

Vibe coding is the practice of building software by describing what you want in plain language — to an AI — and having it write the code. No formal programming knowledge required. You describe the product, the feature, the fix. The AI codes. You review, iterate, describe again.

The term was coined by Andrej Karpathy (formerly OpenAI, formerly Tesla AI) in early 2025, and it spread fast — because it named something a lot of people were already quietly doing. The idea isn't that you never look at code. It's that the barrier between "I have an idea" and "I have a working product" has collapsed.

This is a genuinely big deal.

Software development has always had a translation problem: the person with the idea is rarely the person who writes the code, and something gets lost every time the handoff happens. Vibe coding closes that gap. The product manager, the strategist, the founder, the curious person with a laptop at midnight — they can now build. Not prototype. Not wireframe. Build.

For someone like me — who grew up thinking "I can't code" — this was revelatory. Claude Code (Anthropic's AI coding tool) turns out to be extraordinarily good at this. You describe what you want. It builds it. You push back. It refines. You break things. It fixes them. And slowly, embarrassingly fast, you end up with something real.

The Rabbit Hole

Here's the thing about vibe coding: once you start, you don't stop.

There's a compulsion to it — a creative itch that now has an outlet that actually works. You start with a small thing. You fix it. You improve it. You add a feature. You think: what else can I build? What else can I make better?

I don't fully know where this leads. But I know the feeling of having something to build and the tools to actually build it. That's new for me, and it's addictive in the best possible way.

The first thing I built was this website.

What This Site Is For

This is where I'll write about everything. No fixed format, no single topic. You'll find posts about AI and how it's actually changing organisations — not the hype version, the real friction-filled version. You'll find posts about product management from someone who came to it late and is figuring it out in public. You'll find travel logs, personal reflections, and occasionally something about cooking or padel or FC Barcelona.

The through-line is honesty. I'm not writing from the other side of anything. I'm writing from inside — a new role, a new discipline, a city I love, a dog who doesn't care about my schedule, and a genuine curiosity about what comes next.

If that sounds interesting to you, you're in the right place. Follow along. Push back. Say hello.

Welcome.
