How AI is Reshaping the Consulting Industry From the Inside Out

AI isn't replacing consultants — it's raising the floor of competence and making the value of genuine judgment harder to fake.

DT
Dat Tran
Strategy Consultant · January 22, 2026
Related skills → Client examples →

Last year I ran a workshop with a team of senior partners at a major consulting firm. We spent an hour discussing how AI tools were changing the way their analysts worked. Then one partner said something that stuck with me:

"The problem isn't that AI will make consultants redundant. The problem is that it's going to make it very hard to hide the ones who never added much value in the first place."

That observation cuts to the heart of what's actually happening. The consulting industry is not being disrupted in the way technology disrupts taxi drivers or travel agents. It's being sorted — ruthlessly — by a technology that does the commodity work faster, cheaper, and with fewer complaints about billable hours.

What AI Actually Automates

It's worth being precise about this, because the narrative oscillates between panic and dismissal. Here's what I've seen AI tools meaningfully change in consulting practice over the past two years:

  • Research and synthesis: What used to take a junior analyst two days — compiling market data, reading earnings calls, summarizing analyst reports — now takes an hour with the right tooling.
  • First-draft documents: Strategy slides, board memos, executive summaries. AI can produce a credible first draft that the consultant then edits, rather than writes from scratch.
  • Quantitative analysis: Not replacing full financial models, but dramatically accelerating data cleaning, benchmarking, and sensitivity analysis.
  • Interview synthesis: After stakeholder interviews, AI tools can now organize, tag, and surface patterns from transcripts with surprising accuracy.

These tasks used to represent a significant fraction of junior consultant time — and therefore a significant fraction of client fees. That fraction is compressing fast.

The Floor Is Rising

Here's the uncomfortable implication: AI is raising the floor of what competent consulting looks like. A small team with the right AI tools can now produce the analytical output that used to require a large team.

This is genuinely good for clients. It means more firepower per dollar. It means faster turnaround. It means less of the project budget spent on things that were, frankly, never differentiating.

It's also good for the best consultants — the ones who were always spending their time on judgment, synthesis, and relationship work. AI frees them from the tasks that were below their actual capability.

But it's deeply disruptive for the consulting business model as it's historically operated: large teams, long timelines, and high leverage built partly on analysis that clients couldn't efficiently do themselves.

What AI Cannot (Yet) Do

I've thought carefully about this, and I believe there are a handful of consulting activities where AI is not close to substitution in any near-term timeframe:

  • Political navigation inside client organizations. Knowing which executive to align first, how to frame a recommendation so the CFO doesn't block it, how to build a coalition for change — this is deeply human work.
  • Judgment under genuine ambiguity. When the data points in multiple directions and a recommendation must still be made, the quality of that judgment reflects pattern recognition from real experience — not from training data.
  • Trust-based client relationships. Clients share their most sensitive strategic challenges with people they trust personally. That trust is not a product feature. It's built over time through demonstrated reliability and discretion.
  • Facilitation of difficult conversations. Getting a divided leadership team to align, challenging a CEO's assumptions without losing the relationship — this is a human skill that AI can support but not replace.

How I'm Adapting (Practically)

I started experimenting with AI tools in client work in early 2024. Here's what I've actually changed in how I operate:

Research is now a day-one asset, not a week-two deliverable. I use AI to get to a solid market view before the first client meeting, so I can spend that meeting testing hypotheses rather than gathering information.

I write less, edit more. First drafts of client documents now start from an AI-generated scaffold. I spend my time sharpening the argument and adapting the tone, not starting from a blank slide.

I'm more explicit about where I add value. With AI handling more of the analytical heavy lifting, the work I do that clients can't replicate becomes clearer — and I'm more intentional about positioning and pricing around it.

The Real Risk: Consultants Who Don't Adapt

The consultants who are most at risk aren't those who fear AI. It's those who ignore it. Not because AI will take their jobs — but because their competitors will use AI to deliver the same output in less time at lower cost, and clients will notice.

The firms that will thrive are those that use AI to do more work of the kind that only humans can do — more client time, more workshops, more thinking — rather than using it to deliver the same work cheaper and pocket the margin.

That's a choice. And the firms that make it correctly will be very hard to compete with.
